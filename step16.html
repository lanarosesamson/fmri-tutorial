Step 16 — Interpret the results

In this final step, we explore the group analysis results in FSLeyes, identify the activated brain regions using the Harvard-Oxford atlas, and interpret what these activations tell us about motor function.

1

Understand the group result structure

The group analysis created in Step 15 is stored inside a .gfeat directory.

Inside this directory, FEAT creates one cope*.feat folder for each contrast. 

all_movements.gfeat/
├── cope1.feat/
├── cope2.feat/
├── cope3.feat/
├── cope4.feat/
└── cope5.feat/

Each COPE folder contains the group-level statistical results for one contrast.

COPE 1 Right hand > Left hand
COPE 2 Left hand > Right hand
COPE 3 Right hand
COPE 4 Left hand
COPE 5 Both hands

The group analysis folder contains one cope*.feat directory for each contrast.

One COPE folder = one group-level contrast.
2

Open a group result in FSLeyes

Open FSLeyes from the terminal: 

fsleyes &

First, load the MNI anatomical template: 

/fsl/data/standard/MNI152_T1_2mm_brain.nii.gz

Then open the statistical map from the COPE folder that you want to inspect.

For example, the unthresholded Z-statistic map for COPE 1 is located inside: 

all_movements.gfeat/cope1.feat/stats/zstat1.nii.gz

Red–orange clusters: COPE 1 — Right hand > Left hand. These regions show stronger activity during right-hand movement than during left-hand movement.

Blue–cyan clusters: COPE 2 — Left hand > Right hand. These regions show stronger activity during left-hand movement than during right-hand movement.

Important

In practice, inspect one COPE at a time. Two maps are displayed together here only to make the opposite lateralisation patterns easier to compare.

3

Compare COPE 1 and COPE 2

COPE 1 and COPE 2 directly compare right-hand and left-hand movements.

COPE 1 Right hand > Left hand
Regions showing stronger activity during right-hand movement than during left-hand movement.
COPE 2 Left hand > Right hand
Regions showing stronger activity during left-hand movement than during right-hand movement.

These two contrasts highlight opposite lateralisation patterns. The cerebral hemisphere contralateral to the moving hand generally shows stronger motor activity.

COPE 1 emphasises the left-sided motor response associated with right-hand movement, whereas COPE 2 emphasises the right-sided motor response associated with left-hand movement.
4

Explore the remaining contrasts

COPE 3, COPE 4 and COPE 5 show the activation associated with each motor condition individually.

COPE 3 Right hand
Group activation associated with right-hand movement.
COPE 4 Left hand
Group activation associated with left-hand movement.
COPE 5 Both hands
Group activation associated with simultaneous movement of both hands.

Red–yellow clusters: COPE 3 — Right hand. These clusters represent activity associated with right-hand movement.

Blue–green clusters: COPE 4 — Left hand. These clusters represent activity associated with left-hand movement.

Blue–green–purple clusters: COPE 5 — Both hands. These clusters show activity associated with simultaneous movement of both hands.

The different colours within this map represent different Z-statistic values.

How should the colours be interpreted?

The colour map identifies the displayed statistical overlay. Within one overlay, changes in colour and brightness represent differences in statistical intensity.

5

Use the Harvard-Oxford atlas

A statistical map shows where significant activations are located, but it does not automatically provide the anatomical name of the activated brain region.

To identify these regions, FSLeyes includes the Harvard-Oxford atlas, which overlays anatomical labels onto the statistical maps.

To open the Atlas panel in FSLeyes, follow this menu path: 

Settings → Ortho View 1 → Atlas panel

The Atlas panel appears at the bottom of the FSLeyes window. Open the Atlas information tab.

Enable the atlas you want to query:

Cortical regions

Harvard-Oxford Cortical Structural Atlas

Subcortical regions

Harvard-Oxford Subcortical Structural Atlas

Use the checkboxes beside the atlas names to display information for the current cursor position. You can also use Show/Hide to display the atlas regions as coloured overlays.

In this view, the multicoloured regions represent anatomical labels from the Harvard-Oxford atlas.

Each atlas colour corresponds to a different anatomical brain region. These colours do not represent activation strength and they do not correspond to a particular COPE.

Move the green crosshair onto an activation cluster. The Atlas information panel displays the most likely anatomical regions together with their probabilities.

At the selected coordinate, the atlas indicates approximately 48% Postcentral Gyrus and 25% Precentral Gyrus.

Statistical colours versus atlas colours

Statistical colours represent Z-statistic values from a COPE. Atlas colours represent anatomical brain regions. They must not be interpreted in the same way.

6

Which brain regions are activated?

Across the five contrasts, the group maps show a distributed sensorimotor network involved in voluntary hand movement.

Precentral Gyrus Contains the primary motor cortex. It contributes directly to the execution of voluntary hand movements.
Postcentral Gyrus Contains the primary somatosensory cortex. It processes tactile and proprioceptive feedback produced during movement.
Supplementary Motor Area Contributes to motor planning, movement sequences and the coordination of movements involving both hands.
Premotor Cortex Contributes to movement preparation, selection and organisation before execution.
Cerebellum Contributes to movement timing, precision, coordination and error correction.
Bilateral Sensorimotor Network Especially visible in COPE 5, because simultaneous movement of both hands recruits motor and sensory regions in both hemispheres.

COPE 1 and COPE 2 highlight the relative difference between the right-hand and left-hand conditions.

COPE 3 and COPE 4 show the broader sensorimotor networks associated with right-hand and left-hand movement individually.

COPE 5 shows a more bilateral pattern because both hands are moving simultaneously.

7

Scientific conclusion

The group analysis reveals the expected sensorimotor network associated with voluntary hand movement.

The direct comparisons in COPE 1 and COPE 2 show opposite lateralisation patterns, consistent with the contralateral organisation of the human motor system.

COPE 3 and COPE 4 show the activation associated with right-hand and left-hand movement individually. Their cortical patterns involve the hemisphere contralateral to the moving hand, together with additional sensorimotor and cerebellar regions.

COPE 5 shows the activation associated with simultaneous movement of both hands. This condition produces a more bilateral pattern because both sensorimotor systems are recruited.

The main cortical activations are located around the central sulcus, particularly in the Precentral Gyrus and Postcentral Gyrus. These regions are respectively associated with motor execution and somatosensory feedback.

Additional activation in medial motor areas and the cerebellum is consistent with movement planning, coordination, timing and error correction.

The Harvard-Oxford atlas helps confirm the anatomical locations of the observed clusters and allows the statistical results to be interpreted in a neurobiological context.

Overall, the observed activation patterns are consistent with the known organisation of the human sensorimotor system and support the validity of the analysis pipeline developed throughout this tutorial.
