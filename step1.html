Step 1 — Understand the experiment

Before analysing the data, we need to understand the study, the task and our focus.

What is the original study?

This tutorial is based on a public fMRI study about handedness, motor execution and motor imagery.

The original study compared right-handed and left-handed participants.

It investigated how handedness influences brain activation during hand movements.

What did the participants do?

Participants performed repeated ball-squeezing movements.

The movements were organised into three conditions:

  • Right — squeeze the ball with the right hand
  • Left — squeeze the ball with the left hand
  • Both — squeeze the ball with both hands

The MRI session consisted of four different MRI sequences.

  • T1 structural MRI — a high-resolution anatomical scan of the brain.
  • Motor imagery fMRI — participants imagined squeezing the ball while functional MRI data were acquired.
  • DTI (Diffusion Tensor Imaging) — an MRI technique used to study white matter pathways.
  • Motor execution fMRI — participants physically squeezed the ball while functional MRI data were acquired.

What do we focus on in this tutorial?

In this tutorial, we do not analyse the full study.

We focus on a smaller and simpler part of the dataset:

  • right-handed participants only
  • motor execution only

Our main goal is to compare brain activation during right-hand and left-hand movements.

We also keep the Both condition so that we can learn how to build a complete FEAT model using multiple experimental conditions.

What do we expect to see in the brain?

The brain is organised so that each hemisphere primarily controls voluntary movements on the opposite side of the body.

This is called contralateral motor control.

  • Right-hand movement → stronger activation in the left motor cortex
  • Left-hand movement → stronger activation in the right motor cortex

Some regions may also show bilateral activation, especially during the Both condition.
